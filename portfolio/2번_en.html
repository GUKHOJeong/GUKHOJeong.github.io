2. Challenge (Situation & Task)

üßê Situation

We aimed to build a prediction model for sustainable fisheries by understanding the impact of ocean climate factors on actual fish catches. We attempted a multimodal approach using both satellite imagery (KMA data) and numerical data.

üöß Difficulties

  • High Volatility: The standard deviation of catch data was extremely high, making learning difficult. Changing the metric to 'catch rate' did not resolve the irregularity.
  • Low Prediction Performance: Initial Deep Learning (CNN, LSTM) models showed very high RMSE/MAE error rates.
  • Data Scarcity: The number of training samples was insufficient relative to the model parameters, leading to Overfitting.

3. Process & Action

‚úÖ Hypothesis Testing & Approach

  1. Multimodal Approach: Attempted pattern analysis by combining satellite images (CNN) and time-series numerical data (LSTM).
  2. Dimensionality Reduction & Feature Selection: Removed noise using PCA and domain knowledge-based Feature Selection.
  3. External Factor Analysis: Analyzed the impact of 'Policy and Artificial Fishing Activities' (Outliers) on catch volume, beyond simple climate data.

üîß Troubleshooting (Model Pivot)

To solve the overfitting problem of DL models, we switched to robust Machine Learning Ensemble models (XGBoost, Random Forest) which perform better on smaller datasets.

[ML Models showing stable trends compared to DL Models]

4. Result & Insight

üìä Final Conclusion

  • Limitation of Environmental Variables: The impact of simple ocean environment changes (SST, Salinity) on actual catch was lower than expected.
  • Discovery of Non-linear Factors: Derived the fact that fish catch is more influenced by artificial interventions like Policies (Closed Seasons, Quotas) and Fishing Activities than natural factors.
  • Modeling Lesson: Confirmed that "Choosing a model (ML) suitable for the data scale and characteristics is more beneficial for performance than blindly applying the latest Deep Learning techniques."
